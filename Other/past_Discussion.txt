Discussion: Modeling Emotional Trajectories Over Time
Background
In DREAMS, emotions are not isolated events. They unfold over time and form patterns that are meaningful only when viewed across longer periods rather than as single observations.

A sequence of emotional observations such as early periods dominated by negative emotions, followed by neutral and later positive ones can reveal trends in emotional change without requiring interpretation or explanation.

Proposed Direction
This approach focuses on representing emotions as time-ordered sequences. Emotional observations are organized into timelines that allow aggregation at different temporal resolutions, such as weekly or monthly intervals.

The emphasis is on capturing directionality of change over time whether emotional states appear to be improving, stable, or declining without attempting to explain causality or assign meaning.

What This Enables
By formalizing emotional trajectories, DREAMS can:

Represent emotional evolution in a consistent and reusable structure
Support longitudinal analysis without relying on single snapshots
Provide a foundation for narrative and proximity-based research layers
Scope and Constraints
This work is strictly structural and observational. It does not:

Interpret emotional causes
Predict future emotional states
Perform semantic or psychological analysis
Open Question
Does this framing align with the intended role of temporal emotion modeling in DREAMS, or should the temporal granularity or abstraction be adjusted?

Should we categorise a place by the emotions people feel there?
Hi @pradeeban, I’ve been thinking about something simple but complicated:

Can we describe a place based on the emotions people show there?

Take a railway station. In the morning, it feels rushed and tense. At night, it feels quiet and drained. So is it a “stressful place”? Or does that depend entirely on the moment?

Same with a hospital. You’ll see fear, but also relief. A park might look calm overall, but someone there could be grieving. If we average everyone’s expressions, are we actually capturing the “feeling” of the place, or just flattening human experience into a label?

There’s also a practical angle. If we could understand emotional patterns in spaces, maybe it could help with urban planning, crowd safety, or even mental health support. But it also raises concerns about oversimplification and surveillance.

So I’m curious what others think:

Do places have emotional identities?
Should we treat emotion as something stable enough to classify?
Does aggregation help us understand a space - or distort it?
Where should the ethical boundaries be?

Multimodal Emotion Disagreement Detection, Seeking Feedback on Approach
Hi @pradeeban ,

Building on the existing emotion analysis work in DREAMS, I've been thinking about a specific scenario that I believe has strong clinical relevance and wanted to get your thoughts before proceeding.

The Scenario

When a user uploads a photo, DREAMS currently analyzes:

Facial expression (face emotion ML model)
User caption/description (BERT text model)
Audio voice note (planned)
Sometimes these three signals may contradict each other. For example:

Face shows 80% positive emotion
Caption reads "feeling really low today"
Voice note sounds distressed
My Proposed Approach

Rather than resolving the disagreement by averaging or picking one signal over another, I propose treating the disagreement itself as a clinically meaningful signal.

The system would:

Store all three emotion scores independently
Calculate a disagreement score between modalities
Flag high disagreement entries in the recovery report
- Surface them to healthcare providers as "emotional inconsistency detected"
Why I Think This Matters

In recovery contexts, emotional masking is a real phenomenon. Someone consistently presenting positive facial expressions while writing negative captions over several weeks could be an important signal for healthcare providers, one that would be invisible if we simply averaged the modalities together.

My Question

Does this approach flagging disagreement rather than resolving it align with the clinical goals of DREAMS? Should the disagreement flag be visible only to healthcare providers, or also to the individual user themselves?

Also, do you think emotional masking detection is within the intended scope of DREAMS, or is it too interpretive for the current stage?

Looking forward to your thoughts.

Background Scene Analyser for DREAMS: Anchorage-Grounded Location Understanding

Hi @pradeeban,

I've been thinking about the location proximity challenge and wanted to propose a Background Scene Analyser that is specifically grounded in the real environments DREAMS participants inhabit in Anchorage.

The Problem With Generic Scene Classification

Existing approaches like Places365 are trained on global web-scraped imagery. For DREAMS participants in Anchorage, this creates a fundamental mismatch. A model that has never seen Brother Francis Shelter, Beans Café, the Alaska Native Heritage Center, or Ship Creek encampments will misclassify the exact environments most relevant to recovery in this community. Building an emotion-place pipeline on top of noisy scene labels defeats the purpose entirely.

Proposed Approach

Use Places365 as the pretrained backbone, then fine-tune on a small Anchorage-specific dataset assembled from publicly available sources like Google Street View, Flickr Creative Commons, Municipality of Anchorage open data, and OpenStreetMap tagged POIs. This gives stable, interpretable categories grounded in what DREAMS users actually photograph:

Alaska Native cultural space
Clinical or institutional setting
Faith community space
Recovery support environment
Residential or transitional housing
Shelter or drop-in center
Outdoor / wilderness (Alaska-specific)
The scene classifier never replaces user tags. If a user tagged a photo "hard day," the system now knows that hard day happened in a clinical setting versus a faith space, without interpreting their meaning.

Why Interpretable Categories Matter at DREAMS Scale

With 10-25 participants, unsupervised clustering produces unstable per-user labels impossible to compare across participants. Stable Anchorage-grounded categories let a healthcare provider ask: "Show me all entries from clinical settings this month across participants." That query is only possible with consistent labels.

This approach also classifies scene type entirely from the image with no GPS coordinates stored or transmitted, which addresses the privacy concerns raised in this thread.

It also supports near-duplicate location detection. Two photos from the same place taken weeks apart will share scene type, similar BLIP captions, and visual similarity, giving soft location de-duplication without exact coordinate matching.

Questions for Your Feedback

Do these scene categories reflect the environments DREAMS participants actually document, or are there Anchorage-specific place types I should add?
Would filtering entries by scene type on the provider dashboard be useful, or is that too interpretive for the current stage?
Should domain adaptation use only publicly available imagery, or is there a pathway to incorporate existing DREAMS-consented location data for fine-tuning?
